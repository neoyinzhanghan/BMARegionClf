The term myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) is used to  
describe bilineage or trilineage morphologic dysplastic  
changes in the bone marrow. By definition, MDS is a clonal  
disorder that affects all hematopoietic cell lines. The majori-  
ty of patients with MDS are destined to transform into acute  
leukemia. Myelodysplastic syndromes appearing without  
obvious explanation or cause are called de novo, while those  
developing after exposure to chemicals, radiation, or  
chemotherapeutic agents are called secondary MDS.  
Single or complex cytogenetic abnormalities are found in the  
majority of patients with MDS. They often involve chromo-  
somes 5, 7, and 8 alone or in combination with other defects.  
Treatment  
Treatment of MDS includes support with red blood cell or  
platelet transfusions for patients with refractory anemia or  
thrombocytopenia. Aggressive antibiotic therapy should be  
administered to patients with neutropenic fever. Growth  
factors to maintain appropriate levels of red cells, neu-  
trophils, and platelets have recently become available to help  
support patients with MDS. These measures, however, do  
not alter the course of these patients but provide temporary  
control of the symptoms. Presently, only patients younger  
than 55 years of age can potentially be cured by matched-  
sibling bone marrow transplantation.  
Presentation of MDS  
The majority of patients affected with MDS are above 60  
years of age. Patients usually present with refractory anemia,  
fatigue, and fever secondary to neutropenic infections.  
Thrombocytopenia is common in patients with MDS and  
may lead to frequent transfusions to prevent or treat bleed-  
ing episodes. Splenomegaly occurs in approximately 10-20%  
of patients diagnosed with MDS.
